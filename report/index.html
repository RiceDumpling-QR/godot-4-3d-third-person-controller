RoboBlast · Godot 4.6 · Locomotion recovery benchmark

Can an agent rebuild the feel of movement?

We removed RoboBlast’s complete third-person locomotion system, asked three isolated Claude agents to restore it, and graded observable behavior with a hidden headless verifier.

01 · Task

Remove locomotion, preserve the game.

The ablation removes camera-relative movement, acceleration, stopping, character rotation, grounded and variable-height jumping, locomotion animation, footsteps, and landing feedback. Combat, camera control, enemies, collectibles, HUD, physics, and the level remain intact.

Why this feature:

  • Its loss is immediately visible to a player.
  • A good repair must coordinate input, camera transforms, physics, collision, animation, and audio.
  • Its quality can be measured from behavior without requiring a particular code structure.

04 · Anti-cheat validation

Near misses must not look perfect.

The verifier and reference implementation were hidden from agents. Every run used the same ablated commit, prompt, model setup, isolated worktree, and fresh conversation.

Five patches then introduced one plausible defect into the working game. They do not prevent copying by themselves; they demonstrate that a superficially working implementation cannot fool the behavioral grader.

05 · Three Claude attempts

Same task, different recovery.

Claude 1

83.5/100

Movement, camera relativity, diagonal handling, stopping, orientation, air-jump rejection, variable jump, and landing largely work.

Lost pointsBasic movement 7.5/15; acceleration 7/10; grounded jump 4/10.
Metric evidenceOnly two cardinal alignments passed, speed settled at 6 instead of the intended range, and jump rise was only 0.37 units.

Failure classification: real agent bugs. The measurements directly violate the prompt’s directional, speed, and meaningful-jump requirements.

Claude 2

16/100

The evaluated player produces zero horizontal displacement and no measurable jump arc; the result remains close to the ablated baseline.

Lost pointsAll movement metrics failed; grounded jump received 4/10; stopping and landing received incidental partial credit.
Metric evidenceAll four travel distances and all sampled horizontal speeds were 0. Jump rise was 0 and no landing cycle completed.

Failure classification: real integration failure. The implementation did not produce the observable locomotion requested by the task.

Claude 3

87.5/100

Camera-relative movement, diagonal handling, acceleration, orientation, and the complete jump arc pass. The remaining issues are cardinal alignment and incomplete stopping.

Lost pointsBasic movement 7.5/15; stopping 5/10.
Metric evidenceOnly two cardinal alignments passed. Released movement slowed from 7.77 but retained 0.77 units/s instead of settling near zero.

Failure classification: real agent bugs. The verifier captured direction mismatch and residual drift that map directly to task requirements.

06 · Conclusion

The task reached the intended partial-credit range.

Two agents recovered most of the locomotion stack but missed measurable details; one attempt remained near the broken baseline. The spread from 16 to 87.5 shows that the task is solvable without being trivial, while the original, ablated, and probe results anchor the verifier at both extremes.

Agent bug vs. verifier artifact. The reported agent failures are supported by direct displacement, speed, direction, and jump measurements. During calibration, the verifier itself was also corrected when Godot 4.6’s diagonal input behavior caused the working original to miss an initial threshold; every baseline and probe was then rerun with the final verifier.
